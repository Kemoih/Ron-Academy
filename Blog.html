10 Effective Study Techniques for Online Learning

Online learning presents unique challenges and opportunities. Here are 10 research-backed techniques to maximize your learning:

1. The Pomodoro Technique

Work for 25 minutes, then take a 5-minute break. After four cycles, take a longer 15-30 minute break. This method helps maintain focus and prevents burnout.

2. Active Recall

Instead of passively rereading material, test yourself on the concepts. This strengthens memory pathways more effectively than passive review.

3. Spaced Repetition

Review material at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 1 week, etc.). This leverages the psychological spacing effect for better long-term retention.

4. The Feynman Technique

Explain concepts in simple terms as if teaching someone else. This reveals gaps in your understanding and strengthens comprehension.

5. Interleaving

Mix different subjects or types of problems during study sessions. This improves your ability to discriminate between concepts.

6. Dual Coding

Combine verbal and visual information (words with diagrams) to create multiple memory pathways for the same information.

7. Self-Explanation

As you work through material, explain to yourself how new information relates to what you already know.

8. Concrete Examples

For abstract concepts, create or find concrete examples that illustrate the principle in action.

9. Elaborative Interrogation

Ask "why" questions about the material and answer them to deepen understanding.

10. Retrieval Practice

Regularly practice recalling information without looking at your notes or materials.

References

  • Dunlosky, J., et al. (2013). Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques. Psychological Science in the Public Interest.
  • Brown, P. C., Roediger, H. L., & McDaniel, M. A. (2014). Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning.
  • Oakley, B. (2014). A Mind for Numbers: How to Excel at Math and Science.

Overcoming Math Anxiety: A Student's Guide

Math anxiety affects many students, but with the right strategies, it can be overcome. Here's a comprehensive approach:

Understanding Math Anxiety

Math anxiety is a feeling of tension and fear that interferes with math performance. It's not about ability but about emotional response.

Strategies to Overcome Math Anxiety

1. Reframe Your Mindset

Understand that math ability isn't fixed - it grows with practice. Adopt a growth mindset toward learning math.

2. Start with Basics

Identify and strengthen foundational skills before tackling more complex concepts. Gaps in understanding often cause anxiety.

3. Practice Regularly

Short, frequent practice sessions are more effective than cramming. Consistency builds confidence.

4. Use Multiple Approaches

If one explanation doesn't make sense, seek alternative explanations or visual representations.

5. Develop Problem-Solving Strategies

Learn systematic approaches like: Understand the problem, devise a plan, carry out the plan, look back.

6. Manage Test Anxiety

Practice under timed conditions, use relaxation techniques, and focus on the process rather than the outcome.

7. Connect Math to Real Life

Find applications of math concepts in everyday situations to make them more meaningful.

8. Seek Help Early

Don't wait until you're completely lost. Ask questions when concepts first become unclear.

9. Celebrate Small Wins

Acknowledge progress, no matter how small. Each concept mastered is a step forward.

10. Use Technology Wisely

Educational apps and online resources can provide alternative explanations and practice opportunities.

References

  • Boaler, J. (2016). Mathematical Mindsets: Unleashing Students' Potential through Creative Math.
  • Beilock, S. L. (2011). Choke: What the Secrets of the Brain Reveal About Getting It Right When You Have To.
  • National Council of Teachers of Mathematics. (2014). Principles to Actions: Ensuring Mathematical Success for All.
